Context?
What is the historical setting?
Who is the audience?  What do you know about them?
What was the subject of the previous paragraph?
Digging deeper: Check Bible handbooks/commentaries for more information about the background of the book and/or passage.

General observations or questions?
What are your immediate thoughts or questions as you study this passage?
Any other thoughts or questions you have that do not necessarily fit under the other categories below?
Tip: Don't get bogged down trying to answer every question.

Key terms?
What words seem to stand out as important in this passage?
How are they defined in lexicons/dictionaries?
Any important grammatical aspects (ex. the perfect tense in Greek)?
Note: This category mainly applies to doctrinal books like the Pauline epistles.
Tip:  Bible programs, Bible apps and certain websites (like BlueLetterBible.org) can really help in this area.  Also, check other translations.

Difficulties?
Anything about this passage that you find difficult to understand (or perhaps even troubling)?

Anything seem unusual/unexpected?
What things were said or written that seem unusual, puzzling, unexpected, etc.?
Note: Asking this question can really help point to larger issues beyond those in the passage itself.

Comparisons/contrasts?
Look for explicit or implied comparisons/contrasts between passages and within the passage you are studying.
Note: You will find this throughout Scripture

Cross references?
Look for other passages that mention the same people or subjects.
Tip: The Treasury of Scripture Knowledge is especially helpful here; Bible programs and many Bible websites have this resource.

What does this say or imply about God?
What does this passage say or imply about God's holiness, justice, power, mercy, grace, love, etc.?
Note: It is important for your study to be devotional and not just academic.

Spiritual resources?
What does this passage say or imply about the spiritual resources God has made available to you as His child?

Corrects conduct or teaching?
Does this passage address things that we need to start or stop doing?  Does it correct theological error?
Note: 2 Tim. 3:16 states that Scripture is "profitable for teaching (how to think), for reproof (how not to think), for correction (how not to live), for training in righteousness (how to live)"

Takeaways?
What things have you learned?  What things have you been reminded of?

Application?
How will you personally apply this passage?
